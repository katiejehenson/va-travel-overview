SECTION 01

The lingo

Eighteen terms you'll hit in week one, grouped the way they actually come up — who's involved, what the money's called, and what's on the paperwork.

DMC
Destination Management Company

A local company on the ground in the destination that handles logistics — drivers, guides, restaurant holds, transfers, local fixes. The client may never hear this term, but the DMC is often who actually makes the day-to-day trip happen.

TA
Travel Advisor

The person you're supporting. Builds the itinerary, owns the client relationship, and is who suppliers pay commission to.

IC
Independent Contractor

An advisor who books under a host agency's accreditation rather than running their own agency from scratch. Most solo advisors are ICs.

HOST
Host Agency

Holds the IATA/CLIA accreditation numbers that let independent advisors earn commission, and provides supplier access and back-office support in exchange for a cut of each booking.

SUP
Supplier

Whoever is actually delivering the travel — hotel, cruise line, tour operator, airline, or DMC. The advisor sells the trip; the supplier fulfills it.

PAX
Passenger(s)

Shorthand for the traveler(s) on a booking. "2 PAX, ocean view" tells you everything you need at a glance.

FIT
Free Independent Traveler

A custom trip built around one party's dates, pace, and preferences, rather than slotted into a group departure. This is the trip type behind most of this guide.

GIT
Group Inclusive Travel

A prebuilt itinerary sold to many travelers at once — the opposite of FIT, and a useful contrast for why FIT trips need so much individual coordination.

NET
Net Rate

What the supplier charges the advisor before any markup. The client-facing price is the net rate plus the advisor's margin, or the advisor charges a flat planning fee instead.

COMM
Commission

The percentage — commonly 10 to 20% — a supplier pays the advisor after the trip is taken. It's why advisors can often charge clients little or nothing for the booking itself.

SS
Single Supplement

Most rates assume two people splitting a room; a solo traveler pays this surcharge to cover the difference.

FINAL
Final Payment Date

The deadline for the remaining balance, set well before departure. Miss it and suppliers can cancel the booking — this date drives most of the reminder emails you'll send.

PNR
Passenger Name Record

The unique reference code tied to a flight reservation inside the booking system. It's what "confirmation number" usually means for air.

GDS
Global Distribution System

The backend network (Sabre, Amadeus, Travelport) advisors use to search live fares and inventory and issue tickets — the wholesale side of what you see on an airline's website.

VOU
Voucher

Proof handed to a supplier that a service is already paid for. Present it at the hotel desk or with the guide and the bill's already settled — that's the point.

ETA
Electronic Travel Authorization

A pre-arrival entry permission some countries require before a flight — separate from a visa, usually applied for online. Common enough now to get its own line on the pre-trip checklist.

FAM
Familiarization Trip

A steeply discounted or complimentary trip a supplier offers an advisor so they can experience a hotel or destination firsthand. Selling something you've actually seen beats selling a brochure.

CRM
Trip Management Platform

Where proposals, invoices, forms, and the day-by-day task calendar in this guide actually live (Travel Joy and Tern are common examples). If your advisor already runs one, most of Section 02 below is that system on autopilot.

SECTION 02

The lifecycle of an FIT trip

Eight stages from first contact to six months after the client's home. Timing is shown relative to the trip's start date (T) — this is the rhythm your task list will run on.

STAGE 01

Inquiry

DAY 0

A client reaches out — referral, repeat booking, web form. The first job is simply capturing what's true: dates, destination, party size, rough budget.

Send the welcome email and inquiry form, log the lead in the CRM, and flag it for follow-up if there's no response within 48 hours.

STAGE 02

Discovery call

DAYS LATER

The advisor talks through the trip's real shape — style, pace, must-dos, and what the budget can actually buy.

Get the call on the calendar, send the confirmation and reminder, and prep a one-page summary of anything already on file for the client.

STAGE 03

Proposal & planning fee

WEEK 1–2

The advisor researches and drafts the proposal — hotels, DMC-arranged experiences, flights. Many charge a planning fee here before going further.

Send the planning fee invoice, and once it clears, move the trip's status to Planning in the CRM so the next round of tasks unlocks.

STAGE 04

Agreement & deposit

ON APPROVAL

The client approves the proposal, signs the Travel Services Agreement, and pays a deposit — usually alongside a credit card authorization for future charges.

Send the agreement and CCA form, then confirm both the signature and the deposit have landed before anything gets confirmed with suppliers.

STAGE 05

Booking

T−180 TO T−90

The advisor locks rates and confirms space with every supplier and DMC, issues flights, and sends the client a confirmed itinerary.

Send the booking and trip confirmation emails, and set up the payment schedule with reminders — these are what protect the final payment date.

STAGE 06

Pre-trip operations

T−60 TO T−1

The densest stretch, and mostly yours to run. The advisor designed the trip; the VA executes the calendar below, almost entirely on autopilot once it's set up.

Work the countdown. Each item is destination-specific — nothing here repeats exactly the same way twice, even on a "standard" checklist.

T−60Dining reservations requested, dietary restrictions logged
T−55Vaccinations & visas flagged for a travel doctor
T−40Add-on experiences (photo shoots, etc.) confirmed
T−35Final paperwork requested from every supplier
T−30Currency & pre-departure info sent to client
T−22Countdown document package assembled
T−14Final documents sent — vouchers, itinerary, insurance
T−10Trip app loaded; ETA/visa reminder sent
T−7Packing list sent; hotels reconfirmed directly
T−2Bon voyage email
T−1Flight check-in reminder
STAGE 07

In-trip

T+1

The client is traveling. Day-to-day, things mostly run themselves — until something doesn't.

Send the day-one check-in, keep an eye on incoming messages, and if something goes wrong on the ground, the DMC's local contact is usually the one who actually fixes it — not the desk back home.

STAGE 08

Post-trip

T+2 TO T+6 MONTHS

The trip is over, but the relationship — and the commission — isn't fully closed out yet.

Send the welcome-home and feedback emails, confirm commission was received and logged, and put a six-month check-in on the calendar to stay in front of the client's next trip.

SECTION 03

Three things to remember

The lingo and the timeline will come with repetition. These won't — worth keeping in mind from day one.

On the ground

The DMC fixes it, not you

When something goes sideways mid-trip — a delayed transfer, a closed restaurant — route it to the destination contact. Logistics get solved locally, not from a desk eight time zones away.

On the calendar

Dates drive everything

Most of the job is offset math: counting back from the trip's start date or forward from its end date. Get comfortable with T-minus thinking early — it's the spine of the whole lifecycle above.

On every trip

FIT means custom — assume nothing repeats

Even tasks that look templated, like a packing list or an insurance email, need destination-specific details checked every single time. That's the whole point of "free independent."
